Division in Durham. https://themonoclenow.com/news/one-protest-two-countries-division-in-durham/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=one-protest-two-countries-division-in-durham Mon, 10 Feb 2020 15:06:30 +0000 https://themonoclenow.com/?p=356 The Monocle takes a look at an the Hong Kong protest movement and its relevance to Durham.

The post One Protest Two Countries. Division in Durham. appeared first on The Monocle.

]]> At Durham University Library, Hong Kong protesters stand in a phalanx in silence, black masks over their faces, large cardboard posters in their hands. Students, typically, take no notice. They walk past, generally supportive but ultimately uninterested. However, today is different; opposite the protesters stand a small group of pro-mainland students holding Chinese flags. No words are spoken, but the air fills with a tentative silence. Other students watch on, some in apprehension, others in fascination. The world has come to Durham.

At the grassroot level, these pro-democracy confrontations have spread rapidly across nations that have stayed studiously silent on the protests. In small university towns, thousands of miles from China, pro-democracy protesters are clashing with supporters of Xi Jinping’s government, sometimes violently. Durham, with a population of less than 50,000, is one of these small cities. With perpetual grey skies and more pubs than Uber drivers, Durham has never been an international or multicultural city. However, as in most university towns across the West, this is changing.

Data from the Higher Education Statistics Authority shows that the number of Chinese students at Durham has increased by over 30% in the last four years and they now make up almost ten per cent of the university population. This trend is replicated across the UK: according to HESA, last year 31% of international students across the UK were from China. This trend has transformed these Hong Kong protests from staid, often sparsely attended student rallies, into dangerous and potentially violent confrontations. In Sheffield one student was arrested after a confrontation, in Queensland, Australia, students have received death threats from counter-protesters.

Often these confrontations are less about calls for democracy in Hong Kong than they are about identity. As one protest leader in Durham explained: “in order to differentiate themselves they try to build up this HK identity, and this whole prejudice is emerging.” These identities, built on nationalism, have served to inhibit dialogue and entrench positions on both sides.

However, the battle-lines are not as neatly drawn as people may think. “Talking to a lot of people helps break down the stereotype of Chinese people as embracing the communist party” one Hong Kong protester added. “I’ve met loads of people with different opinions on the Chinese government”. Often Chinese students are reticent to state their opinions in public, mindful of the dangers, fearful of whom they can trust. One student protester explained that he knew many mainland Chinese who were critical of their government but as he put it mildly, they “would be a bit wary” of voicing those beliefs.

As is often the case, dialogue between the groups, both formal and informal, has engendered understanding and amongst some, acceptance. As one Hong Kong protester reflected: “So I think a lot of people were wary of us, but through talking we have managed to convince a few that our objectives are peaceful … even to Chinese people we shouldn’t be seen as enemies.”

Thinking about her proudest moment she told me, full of modesty, “I suppose it was being chosen as what was that called? Visit England Superstar. That was probably it.”

In fact, in 2016 she won Durham Tourism Superstar and later in the same year the national equivalent, Visit England Superstar. When I asked her who her favourite visitor was, she replied cautiously: “It’s hard to say. I’ve taken Sting who told me that he wanted to adopt me as his mother. I’ve taken Princess Anne, she was terrific fun, marvellous sense of humour.”

However, her favourite was a local woman from Sunderland. Lilian explained that “she came on a public tour and at the end of the tour she crossed my hands and said, ‘flower I wouldn’t have missed that for a million pounds.’ And I thought that was brilliant, absolutely brilliant.”

]]> 350 Inside the Hong Kong Protests https://themonoclenow.com/news/inside-the-hong-kong-protests/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=inside-the-hong-kong-protests Sun, 24 Nov 2019 14:59:17 +0000 https://themonoclenow.com/?p=315 The Monocle sits down with participants in the Hong Kong protests in Durham. ]]> The Monocle sits down with participants in the Hong Kong protests in Durham.

The protests in Hong Kong have been unlike any other in recent memory. Geographically they may be far away. However, they have ignited protest organically in universities across the globe from Brisbane to Bristol.

Just recently a total of 78 Hong Kong-affiliated student groups from across the globe released a Joint Statement on Police Brutality and Academic Freedom. The full list of signatories includes groups in the Netherlands, Los Angeles, New Zealand and of course Durham.

At the Monocle we sat down with members of the Durham Hong Kong protests to understand how this international phenomenon arose and what it is all about.

How the protests came about

The organisers were initially very clear to emphasise that there was no central chain of command linking these protest groups from across the world.

“It’s not centralised at all, it’s very similar to the movement itself back home, there is no central platform, it is all people coming out and saying: we don’t want this.”

The decentralisation of the movement is more than merely circumstantial, in many ways it seems to be what has made them so incredibly effective.

“When I have signed up to be a helper in a London protest, I am not notified about anything until last minute and everything still works out fine. I think this not only represents how the leaderless thing works, but how it has become the characteristic of HK people all over the world.”

“It shows that not everything is centralised, and everyone has their own say in the whole movement as well.”

Dealing with counter-protests

The protests have been met with some opposition and there have been some incidents in which Hong Kong protesters have come into contact with counter-protesters.

The protesters mentioned an incident which took place at Sheffield University where one man was arrested in a counter-protest under suspicion of committing a public order offence.

After this interview took place, there was a counter demonstration by Chinese supporters to the Hong Kong protesters. The atmosphere was tense.

“We know there are risks of counter protesters, but we believe dialogue is the most important thing. The fact we are lucky enough to study at university and have this opportunity whereas a lot of places around the world don’t have this space for dialogue. Therefore, it is important to talk to as many people as possible even though we know they have different preconceptions of our movement.”

What is it like in Hong Kong?

Whilst English universities have supported the Hong Kong protestors’ right to free speech, and Durham is no exception, the reality in Hong Kong as most know is very different.

“Often the running joke is, because many of the people in the protests are quite young, often secondary school students, and the skill which they’ve learnt over the summer is the ability to diffuse teargas in a few seconds. That’s not the experience some of us have but it’s the idea that you will be affected by teargas one way or the other. You can be coming off work, or having a meal with somebody, and you’ll see teargas going off somewhere.”

“It’s something ridiculous like 80% of all HK people have been affected by teargas.”

What do they want?

Often people assume that what the Hong Kong protests are all about is independence from China, but the people we talked to were all quick to emphasise that this is far from their main goal

“The line of argument often going against the Hong Kong movement is that HK is trying to get away from China, it’s a territorial dispute, but no this is absolutely not what we are trying to do.”

In fact, what unites the protests all around the world as well as Hong Kong are five relatively simple demands:

  1. Formal withdrawal of the extradition bill.
  2. Exoneration and release of arrested protesters.
  3. Independent commission and enquiry into police brutality .
  4. The government to withdraw the use of the word “riot” in relation to protests.
  5. Implementation of genuine universal suffrage.

Ultimately, it seems that the protests in Durham and around the world are about exercising their respective right to free speech in support of those whose right is under threat.

“University is and should be a place of freedom of speech and expression it is a very good place to show what this is about and show that in fact free speech and expression isn’t always the case around the world nowadays as we see in Hong Kong.”

]]> A recent meeting of Durham County Council has listed daytime drinking, ‘aggressive begging’ and late night noise in the city centre amongst the main antisocial problems facing the area.

At the meeting, attendees were told that police have stopped giving out community protection orders to students who have been the subject of noise complaints. It was deemed that leaving students with a potential criminal record due to noise complaints was an ‘inappropriate’ use of the law.

In terms of new strategies, police agreed to trial a new tactic of having uniformed officers knock on doors whilst the prospect of more night-time wardens in problematic areas was also considered. Moreover, the idea of student volunteers performing similar duties on the streets was discussed.

Durham University declined to be part of the panel, which included various members from local politics and the wider civic community. Despite this, the university’s pro-vice chancellor Jeremy Cook and community liaison officer Hannah Shepherd both took part in the conference. Students’ Union president Kate McIntosh was also in attendance.

Amongst the topics discussed was the possibility of a bespoke alcohol licensing policy being introduced for Durham City. This comes amidst complaints from residents about the ineffectiveness of the current response to antisocial behaviour, which they claim is doing little to combat the sleepless nights caused by noisy students.

Overall, it was agreed that there could be no quick fix to the issues being faced and that different sectors of the community working together was the only way forward.

The unusual project included the building of a mosquito laboratory and the refurbishment of a separate laboratory (Laboratory 5) in the Biological Science. The firm provided significant assistance in areas such as design and architecture.

The firm have already shown their 40 years experience in working with these complex scientific constructions, having been involved with the relocation of a locust house at Newcastle University, meaning they were well placed when it came to competing for the mosquito laboratory and Laboratory 5.

Durham University hope to support their research staff through promoting invertebrate research and developing appropriately modern facilities.
