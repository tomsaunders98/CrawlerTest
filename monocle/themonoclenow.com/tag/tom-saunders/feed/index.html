]]> At the Monocle we have been tracking how many Coronavirus tests have been performed for 33 different countries around the world. Testing for Covid-19 is often the mark by which a country’s response to the crisis is measured.

We have a simple message for all countries: test test test. Test every suspected case.

World Health Organization (WHO) head Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus

Matt Hancock says: 100,000 tests a day by end of April

Matt Hancock recently said that by the end of the month he hopes to have scaled up testing to 100,000 tests a day. On the face of it this seems like a tall order for a country which to date has only managed to administer slightly less than 300,000 tests.

The green line represents what 100,000 tests per day would look like, whilst the red line shows the UK’s current testing output since the 30th of March.

What is clear from this graph is that the daily increase in testing is only a fraction of the 100,000 goal. The gradient of the UK’s line looks almost flat in comparison to what 100,000 tests per day looks like. Indeed, from the 9th to the 10th of April, the UK only performed 16,095 tests which is nowhere near the 100,000 target.

It remains to be seen whether the UK can pull off such a huge increase over the next few weeks, although the initial data is not very encouraging.

Who is doing the most testing?

Whilst the UK may be struggling to increase its testing output, other countries have scaled up their diagnostics and production capabilities to wartime levels.

Germany has the second highest testing output of any country monitored, even though their data is over a week old. Whilst the UK does fairly well in this graph it must be noted that France’s data is also a week out of date compared to most countries and, even then, the UK is only marginally in front.  

Who is doing the best testing?

This is helpful to show which countries have the largest diagnostic and testing capabilities but a lot of the countries that the UK comes above have populations which are far smaller than the UK and so do not need to do anywhere near the amount of testing.

When adjusting for population the UK’s situation looks far worse:

Clearly, the UK’s testing output so far leaves a lot to be desired.

This also explains why some countries have a lower mortality rate than the United Kingdom. Countries such as Germany can afford to implement widespread testing; if you are suspected of having coronavirus in Germany you can quite easily get a test. However in the UK, due to the shortage of tests, only the most serious cases are tested which then inflates the overall mortality rate.

What about Japan?

According to Reuters Japan is only using 1/6th of its testing capacity. It is not exactly clear why Japan’s test output is so incredibly low compared to other developed nations.

Some have speculated that part of the reason is that companies and government bodies have been reluctant to take aggressive measures to combat the spread of the virus which may lead to heavy losses and an economic downturn.

It seems that aggressive testing is one of these measures that the government is unwilling to pursue.

If we test more will the Lockdown by over sooner ?

Austria, Denmark and the Czech Republic have all announced plans to start easing social distancing restrictions.

There are many factors which go into beating the virus but it is interesting that all three countries have completed far more tests per capita than the UK.

Denmark has announced that schools and day care centres will reopen on the 15th of April and the Czech Republic has announced that it will expand the type of shops that are allowed to remain open. Austria has also announced that it will look to reopen some shops by the 14th April.

All three countries test far more people comparatively than the UK. The UK has indicated that the lockdown will almost certainly be extended. If the UK’s testing output is anything to go by, it might be extending for a whole lot longer.

]]> At Durham University Library, Hong Kong protesters stand in a phalanx in silence, black masks over their faces, large cardboard posters in their hands. Students, typically, take no notice. They walk past, generally supportive but ultimately uninterested. However, today is different; opposite the protesters stand a small group of pro-mainland students holding Chinese flags. No words are spoken, but the air fills with a tentative silence. Other students watch on, some in apprehension, others in fascination. The world has come to Durham.

At the grassroot level, these pro-democracy confrontations have spread rapidly across nations that have stayed studiously silent on the protests. In small university towns, thousands of miles from China, pro-democracy protesters are clashing with supporters of Xi Jinping’s government, sometimes violently. Durham, with a population of less than 50,000, is one of these small cities. With perpetual grey skies and more pubs than Uber drivers, Durham has never been an international or multicultural city. However, as in most university towns across the West, this is changing.

Data from the Higher Education Statistics Authority shows that the number of Chinese students at Durham has increased by over 30% in the last four years and they now make up almost ten per cent of the university population. This trend is replicated across the UK: according to HESA, last year 31% of international students across the UK were from China. This trend has transformed these Hong Kong protests from staid, often sparsely attended student rallies, into dangerous and potentially violent confrontations. In Sheffield one student was arrested after a confrontation, in Queensland, Australia, students have received death threats from counter-protesters.

Often these confrontations are less about calls for democracy in Hong Kong than they are about identity. As one protest leader in Durham explained: “in order to differentiate themselves they try to build up this HK identity, and this whole prejudice is emerging.” These identities, built on nationalism, have served to inhibit dialogue and entrench positions on both sides.

However, the battle-lines are not as neatly drawn as people may think. “Talking to a lot of people helps break down the stereotype of Chinese people as embracing the communist party” one Hong Kong protester added. “I’ve met loads of people with different opinions on the Chinese government”. Often Chinese students are reticent to state their opinions in public, mindful of the dangers, fearful of whom they can trust. One student protester explained that he knew many mainland Chinese who were critical of their government but as he put it mildly, they “would be a bit wary” of voicing those beliefs.

As is often the case, dialogue between the groups, both formal and informal, has engendered understanding and amongst some, acceptance. As one Hong Kong protester reflected: “So I think a lot of people were wary of us, but through talking we have managed to convince a few that our objectives are peaceful … even to Chinese people we shouldn’t be seen as enemies.”

]]> 356 In the Alps: the Source of Knowledge. https://themonoclenow.com/politics/in-the-alps-the-source-of-knowledge/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=in-the-alps-the-source-of-knowledge Thu, 30 Jan 2020 15:01:00 +0000 https://themonoclenow.com/?p=353 The Monocle speaks to one of the foremost experts in AI on the "open source" phenomenon.

The post In the Alps: the Source of Knowledge. appeared first on The Monocle.

]]> In a small research centre nestled in the Alps in south-western Switzerland, in 2002 a group of researchers released an Artificial Intelligence framework free to the public. They released the framework as “Open Source”, meaning that the framework was not only free to use, but the underlying code was available for anyone to learn from it, adapt it or copy it.

The framework enjoyed lasting popularity; its successor, PyTorch, is used by Facebook and the lead developer of the framework, Samy Bengio, is now a research scientist at Google. Mr Bengio explained that when his team released Torch: “we were in a very academic environment, so open sourcing seemed totally legitimate and useful for reproducibility.”

The Open Source model has been a key part of the academic community for a long time. In 2001, 40 members of the editorial board of the Artificial Intelligence journal Machine Learning resigned, explaining that in the era of the Internet it was wrong for researchers to publish their findings in expensive pay-access archives.

This mindset has found its way into big technology firms who are desperate to hire leading academics in the field of Artificial Intelligence: “Publishing and open-sourcing are both part of a healthy scientific community” added Mr Bengio, “Good researchers in our field know that, and they also know that they can somehow choose their employer more than their employer can choose them.” The list of companies that have embraced the Open Source movement in relation to Artificial Intelligence reads like a Who’s Who of the Tech World, Google, Intel, Facebook, Uber, Lyft, Microsoft and even DARPA have contributed to the dizzying quantity of freely available AI tools.

Academics have been able to keep AI a largely open research area because they see the Open Source model as a way to ensure that the field moves as quickly as possible; that academics are able to build on each other’s work as quickly and efficiently as possible.

However, there are risks. The implications of AI advancements have potentially world-shattering consequences. AI, Mr Bengio acknowledged, “could also help bad actors build problematic tools that could be used to harm society. Think for instance about face recognition technology used by rogue governments.”

Unfortunately, it seems that this worst-case scenario has already become a reality. Last week, it was reported that Mr Ton-That, a college drop out and one-time model, used freely available academic papers and open sourced AI products to perfect a Facial Recognition algorithm. It was used in concert with billions of photos scraped from social networks to create a program that can be used to identify anyone from one single photo. His company, Clearview, has licensed his product out to Police departments across the United States.

Google and Microsoft both refrained from open sourcing their facial recognition algorithms for fear of this very thing happening. However, the overwhelming quantity of freely available products and expertise meant that their efforts were ultimately in vain. It’s unclear whether the open source movement will continue in the same vain but whether it does is unlikely to affect AI in the near future. Even products such as Google’s AI text-to-speech generator, which was debuted to much fanfare in 2018 at Google’s annual conference, has been recreated in open source by programmers even though the real thing remains propriety. What this means for the tech world is unclear, but its consequences could be dire. Hackers have already used AI to impersonate the CEO of a U.K.-based energy firm, running away with €220,000. When asked about whether large technology firms will be as committed to open source in AI as they are now, Mr Bengio replied: “I’m not really good at reading the future :-).”

]]> The Monocle sits down with participants in the Hong Kong protests in Durham.

The protests in Hong Kong have been unlike any other in recent memory. Geographically they may be far away. However, they have ignited protest organically in universities across the globe from Brisbane to Bristol.

Just recently a total of 78 Hong Kong-affiliated student groups from across the globe released a Joint Statement on Police Brutality and Academic Freedom. The full list of signatories includes groups in the Netherlands, Los Angeles, New Zealand and of course Durham.

At the Monocle we sat down with members of the Durham Hong Kong protests to understand how this international phenomenon arose and what it is all about.

How the protests came about

The organisers were initially very clear to emphasise that there was no central chain of command linking these protest groups from across the world.

“It’s not centralised at all, it’s very similar to the movement itself back home, there is no central platform, it is all people coming out and saying: we don’t want this.”

The decentralisation of the movement is more than merely circumstantial, in many ways it seems to be what has made them so incredibly effective.

“When I have signed up to be a helper in a London protest, I am not notified about anything until last minute and everything still works out fine. I think this not only represents how the leaderless thing works, but how it has become the characteristic of HK people all over the world.”

“It shows that not everything is centralised, and everyone has their own say in the whole movement as well.”

Dealing with counter-protests

The protests have been met with some opposition and there have been some incidents in which Hong Kong protesters have come into contact with counter-protesters.

The protesters mentioned an incident which took place at Sheffield University where one man was arrested in a counter-protest under suspicion of committing a public order offence.

After this interview took place, there was a counter demonstration by Chinese supporters to the Hong Kong protesters. The atmosphere was tense.

“We know there are risks of counter protesters, but we believe dialogue is the most important thing. The fact we are lucky enough to study at university and have this opportunity whereas a lot of places around the world don’t have this space for dialogue. Therefore, it is important to talk to as many people as possible even though we know they have different preconceptions of our movement.”

What is it like in Hong Kong?

Whilst English universities have supported the Hong Kong protestors’ right to free speech, and Durham is no exception, the reality in Hong Kong as most know is very different.

“Often the running joke is, because many of the people in the protests are quite young, often secondary school students, and the skill which they’ve learnt over the summer is the ability to diffuse teargas in a few seconds. That’s not the experience some of us have but it’s the idea that you will be affected by teargas one way or the other. You can be coming off work, or having a meal with somebody, and you’ll see teargas going off somewhere.”

“It’s something ridiculous like 80% of all HK people have been affected by teargas.”

What do they want?

Often people assume that what the Hong Kong protests are all about is independence from China, but the people we talked to were all quick to emphasise that this is far from their main goal

“The line of argument often going against the Hong Kong movement is that HK is trying to get away from China, it’s a territorial dispute, but no this is absolutely not what we are trying to do.”

In fact, what unites the protests all around the world as well as Hong Kong are five relatively simple demands:

  1. Formal withdrawal of the extradition bill.
  2. Exoneration and release of arrested protesters.
  3. Independent commission and enquiry into police brutality .
  4. The government to withdraw the use of the word “riot” in relation to protests.
  5. Implementation of genuine universal suffrage.

Ultimately, it seems that the protests in Durham and around the world are about exercising their respective right to free speech in support of those whose right is under threat.

“University is and should be a place of freedom of speech and expression it is a very good place to show what this is about and show that in fact free speech and expression isn’t always the case around the world nowadays as we see in Hong Kong.”

]]> Last month the Nation Student Money survey was released by Save the Student. Now in its sixth year it polled 3,385 students, making it the UK’s largest independent survey of its kind.

The report had some dire revelations, most noticeably that 79% of students polled worry about making ends meet – which is more students than ever before. Only 38% of students said that their maintenance loans stretched far enough.

Not all students are able to supplement their maintenance loan with money from their parents or an overdraft. Furthermore, contrary to what people outside university might think, many students simply don’t have time to take on a part-time job in addition to their studies.

This leaves students with few options in order to make ends meet. This is where commercial or payday loan companies come into play. The survey noted that the popularity of payday loans has increased from 2018 to 2019.

These payday loans are often exploitative, and many have begun to directly target cash-strapped students.

Many student unions in the UK have banned short-term lends from advertising on campus and have made active efforts to instead promote hardship funds instead.

When you type “Student Loan quick” into Google plenty of results come up. Many advertise loans from £100-£5,000 which can be agreed and paid in 15 minutes. One of the most prominent of these pay-day lenders is Smart Pig which describes itself as “the only short-term loan created just for students.”

Smart Pig is a perfect example of how an offshoot of the already morally questionable pay-day loan industry deliberately attempts to influence vulnerable students into loans which will often lead them into more debt.

On the page “Our Story”, Smart Pig mentions how the company is a student start up and that their founders “have a strong ethical focus …  and really want to improve practices and reduce prices in the short-term loan industry.” It is unclear exactly what Smart Pig means when it talks of its “strong ethical focus”.

The company has already been in trouble for advertising on beer mats, on the basis that it connected payday loans with drinking. It also was forced to scrap an ‘irresponsible’ poster offering a £3,500 prize because it encouraged students to take on debt. The poster told students that “Every customer is entered into our competition to win a term’s rent up to £3,500.”

Its founders, former Goldman Sachs trader and WBFF Amateur Fitness model Tom Parks and Shreiff Benazina, also own another short-term loan company called Buffa Loans. Buffa Loans features a picture of a mother and child prominently on its landing page and advertises loans with a representative APR of 1087%, which is more than double the 448% APR of Smart Pig. For comparison the APR on fixed rate credit cards is around 13% and for variable rate credit cards around 16%.

In a article in the Financial Times, Mr Benazina explained that “We don’t just want to do loans,” says Mr Benazina. “We want to help people find accommodation and practice for job interviews.” However, at the time of saying that Mr Benazina had been a Director of Buffa Loans, for over two years and seemed to be doing exactly that. The Smart-Pig About Page makes no mention anywhere of their other payday loan ventures and clearly attempts to appear as a solely student focused venture created by good natured students when in reality, they are quite far from that.

If you, or anyone you know, are facing financial difficulties or are unable to pay back a loan from a pay day lender you can contact your local Citizen’s Advice Bureau for help or take a look at DebtCamel which has advice about payday loans and how you may be able to apply for compensation. Durham University and some colleges also have financial support and hardship funds available to students, you can find more information here.

]]> A monocle investigation into sexual misconduct cases at Durham University can reveal that between 2014 and December of 2018, 44% of Accused Parties in cases of sexual misconduct or violence left the University before their hearing.

In the event that an incident of sexual misconduct or violence occurs against a student at Durham, the student essentially has three options: they can either report the incident to the Police, to the University or not at all.

The University disclosed that within the timeframe previously stipulated (2014 and December of 2018) only 39% of cases were reported to Police even though it was assessed that 83% of cases which were reported could also have constituted  a criminal offence. This reluctance to pursue criminal reports is understandable since a criminal trial l can be an immense emotional strain on a victim of sexual misconduct or violence. Not to mention that the high burden of proof (“beyond reasonable doubt”) makes securing a conviction  difficult. Of the cases reported to the police where Durham was informed of the status of the criminal proceedings 75% had ended in No Further Action.

The second option available to students is to report the incident to the University. This is the option that victims who wish to report usually take. Furthermore, since the standard of proof is not the same as the legal standard it is generally a more approachable option. The University even states within its guidance on reporting sexual assault that a decision by the Police or Crown Prosecution Service to take no further action in relation to a criminal matter or an acquittal at a trial does not preclude the University from taking action.

However, there is a significant flaw within the University’s procedure of dealing with sexual assault cases. Students who want to avoid punishment simply have to leave the University and it seems that a shockingly high number of students have been doing just that. Even more concerning is the fact that it is primarily only those students that are accused of the most serious offences which cause students to leave the University, escaping any form of punishment.

Specifically, in the most serious cases of Sexual Assault at Durham University, also known as “Category 2” offences, the perpetrator is referred to the Senate Discipline Committee. These offences consist of the most serious in the opinion of Durham such as rape, abusive sexual behaviour and recording without consent. However, the University has disclosed that between 2014 and December of 2018, 44% of those who referred to the Senate Discipline Committee instead withdrew from the University before the hearing could be held. In these cases, the victim will have no other outcome since the University will drop the disciplinary proceedings if the Accused Party withdraws. At the same time, the perpetrator can presumably enroll at a different university with a relatively unblemished record.

The University also disclosed in their report that “this is a trend that has been highlighted at conferences across the sector in the last year”. However, after The Monocle sent Freedom of Information to every Russel Group university, we can reveal that of all the Universities that responded, Durham had by far the highest rate of students leaving before the end of a university investigation. Birmingham, Cardiff, Belfast did not reply with a sufficient answer and UCL Warwick and Bristol either did not reply at all or did so in an untimely manner.

The University was asked to comment by the Monocle but did not respond in a timely manner.
