I WATCHED THE QUEEN’S SPEECH SO YOU DON’T HAVE TO…

By Conor Johnson on October 14, 2019

…See our reigning monarch pretend that she has faith and trust in her government and prime minister, as if they didn’t lie to her about a certain prorogation of parliament just a couple of weeks ago, that is. Nonetheless, the Queen did a stand-up job at acting like someone who has genuine belief in the current crop of politicians at the top of the Tory party, reminding viewers of the best that this country has to offer. Charles at her side, on the other hand, acted as a reminder that once Queen Elizabeth is gone, so too will be her brand of expert professionalism and diplomacy. Some more discerning readers may have noticed, as every other news outlet has done, that the Queen opted for a slightly more relaxed look this afternoon, donning the state diadem, rather than the state crown, while she delivered Boris Johnson’s plans for this session of parliament. Whether this was a subtle dig at Bozza or merely a necessity for an elderly lady with a weaker neck than it once was is open for debate.

The speech itself touched on all manner of things that Boris and co plan on achieving in parliament, but with the majority (or lack thereof) that Boris commands it is unlikely any of these pledges will actually be achieved anytime soon, causing many to dismiss the speech as an election manifesto rather than a reasonable plan of action. In this sense it is rather uncomfortable seeing the Queen, who is obliged to attend events such as these, being used in what was effectively a glorified launch of an election campaign.

The first line alone outlines what most believe to be a completely unachievable goal; to leave the European Union on the 31st of October. If Boris could manage to convince the other 27 member states of the EU of his nationalist plan to depart from everyone’s favourite project of abolishing the nation state under the guise of economic prosperity, then hats off to him.  Meanwhile, in the real world, Boris continues to make enemies everywhere he goes, and his deal looks no more likely to be approved than Dominic Cummings being recruited to stand for the Liberal Democrats. With ‘No Deal’ widely accepted to be off the cards, in part due to the ‘Surrender/Benn Act’ (delete where appropriate) it looks as though the main policy that Her Majesty’s government is committed to is an unachievable one.

The rest of the Queen’s speech outlines a new vision for the United Kingdom outside of the European Union. Policies on fisheries, trade and agriculture mark the departure from the single market and a new immigration bill to end free movement shows a commitment to change following Britain’s (eventual) exit from the European Union. The speech mentions everything from tougher criminal sentences for those convicted of the most serious crimes to cameos from Carrie Symond’s own playbook such as a new bill promising tougher sentences for animal abuse. All in all, it looks like our prisons will be nice and busy by Christmas.

More progressivism can be seen with the Environment Bill enshrining, for the first time, environmental protection in law – that’s one for the crusties.

This speech then, while acting as a list of unachievable pledges given the current parliamentary composition, is not overly revelatory. That Boris plans on being tough on crime yet fairer on the NHS come as no surprise after the announcements he has made in the last few weeks. Boris told the commons that he plans on governing as a One Nation Tory, and if he can get the Queen’s speech approved by parliament then maybe he will. However, with that prospect looking ever unlikely we may have to wait a bit longer before Johnson gets the chance to enact his vision on our country.
