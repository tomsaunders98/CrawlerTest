]]> The royal family is always a difficult, but popular subject. What should be their role in society? Should they even have a role? Harry and Meghan are refreshingly honest in what they believe: they do not skirt around the truth, and have even allowed us to witness their human side. It is easy to see their hatred of the tabloid culture that is growing increasingly abhorrent in today’s society, and their desire for privacy. They appear to have been ostracised by the royal family for their latest endeavours, losing their HRH titles. Less than affectionately called “Megxit” by the tabloids, the media have showered Meghan with hate and blame. But what does this tell us about the royal family today?

The public eye has been a difficult place for Meghan Markle, with the media grabbing any information they could with eager hands, and beady eyes. It has obviously also taken a toll upon Harry, a man who could not escape the media especially after the tragic death of his mother, when he was a child. The tabloids lap up any “Diana” references they can, but prefer to forget the trauma they subjected Harry and William. The footage of the two boys walking behind her hearse is heart-breaking, and one that symbolises the debacle, particularly as Princess Diana’s car was speeding to escape the paparazzi. The personal perspective was lost in a media frenzy. So, when Harry moves toward privacy and family life, it is an understandable move. But, Meghan is inevitably blamed. As a woman new to the royal family, she is an easy target for the tabloids, and there have been a number of racist connotations toward her. They have also disrupted her private family relationships, publicly involving her father in a number of personal issues that have now been put in print.

It has always been an accepted fact that the media will scrutinise our royal family. However, it is debatable whether this is scrutiny, or just abuse. Whilst this is a boundary that the media have pushed for centuries, it is becoming increasingly personal, particularly in a media culture that depends upon “clicks”. Harry and Meghan’s latest move- to tell the tabloids they will no longer work with them- is laudable. But, simultaneously, it also seems like something that is long overdue. They should be applauded for their latest move, as it will finally give them some semblance of normal life. Harry and Meghan are not destined for Britain’s throne, and if they are aggressively hounded by the media whilst living their lives in Britain, why stay?

It will always be a scandal for a member of the royal family to desire privacy, and, in particular, to move abroad. It is far too a familiar scene that led to King Edward VIII’s abdication in 1936. Despite this being nearly a century ago, it remains infamous for the media and the royal family. They appear desperate to retain their position and earn forgiveness for the mishap of Edward VIII, who is amazingly remembered for his abdication more than his alliance with the Nazis. The media seem to be desperate for a scandal of these proportions and will go to some lengths to try and reinvent one in a world that is far removed from it. Particularly, a world that is battling a global pandemic.

If Meghan and Harry want to renounce their titles and live in peace, it should not matter. They are hardly the heirs apparent. However, the drama over it suggests that there is deep-seated insecurity of the supposed instability of the Windsor dynasty, one that is recognised by the royal family and media alike. They are desperate to have royals remain royal. Princess Anne did not allow her children, Peter Phillips, and Zara Tindall, to inherit her title, yet they remain very much in the public eye. It seems that we will never escape the obsession towards the royal family. No matter how far removed a prince may be, he is still a prince. Princes in fairy-tales always get their happy ending, but not in Britain. Here, they see their wife abused in the media, private communications leaked, pictures of his baby plastered everywhere, and constant reminders of the traumatic loss of his mother. The media needs to remember that behind every royal wave, there is a person.

Matt Hancock says: 100,000 tests a day by end of April

Matt Hancock recently said that by the end of the month he hopes to have scaled up testing to 100,000 tests a day. On the face of it this seems like a tall order for a country which to date has only managed to administer slightly less than 300,000 tests.

The green line represents what 100,000 tests per day would look like, whilst the red line shows the UK’s current testing output since the 30th of March.

What is clear from this graph is that the daily increase in testing is only a fraction of the 100,000 goal. It remains to be seen whether the UK can pull off such a huge increase over the next few weeks, although the initial data is not very encouraging.

]]> 373 In the Alps: the Source of Knowledge. https://themonoclenow.com/politics/in-the-alps-the-source-of-knowledge/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=in-the-alps-the-source-of-knowledge Thu, 30 Jan 2020 15:01:00 +0000 https://themonoclenow.com/?p=353 The Monocle speaks to one of the foremost experts in AI on the "open source" phenomenon.

The post In the Alps: the Source of Knowledge. appeared first on The Monocle.

]]> In a small research centre nestled in the Alps in south-western Switzerland, in 2002 a group of researchers released an Artificial Intelligence framework free to the public. They released the framework as “Open Source”, meaning that the framework was not only free to use, but the underlying code was available for anyone to learn from it, adapt it or copy it.

The framework enjoyed lasting popularity; its successor, PyTorch, is used by Facebook and the lead developer of the framework, Samy Bengio, is now a research scientist at Google. Mr Bengio explained that when his team released Torch: “we were in a very academic environment, so open sourcing seemed totally legitimate and useful for reproducibility.”

The Open Source model has been a key part of the academic community for a long time. In 2001, 40 members of the editorial board of the Artificial Intelligence journal Machine Learning resigned, explaining that in the era of the Internet it was wrong for researchers to publish their findings in expensive pay-access archives.

This mindset has found its way into big technology firms who are desperate to hire leading academics in the field of Artificial Intelligence: “Publishing and open-sourcing are both part of a healthy scientific community” added Mr Bengio, “Good researchers in our field know that, and they also know that they can somehow choose their employer more than their employer can choose them.” The list of companies that have embraced the Open Source movement in relation to Artificial Intelligence reads like a Who’s Who of the Tech World, Google, Intel, Facebook, Uber, Lyft, Microsoft and even DARPA have contributed to the dizzying quantity of freely available AI tools.

Academics have been able to keep AI a largely open research area because they see the Open Source model as a way to ensure that the field moves as quickly as possible; that academics are able to build on each other’s work as quickly and efficiently as possible.

However, there are risks. The implications of AI advancements have potentially world-shattering consequences. AI, Mr Bengio acknowledged, “could also help bad actors build problematic tools that could be used to harm society. Think for instance about face recognition technology used by rogue governments.”

Unfortunately, it seems that this worst-case scenario has already become a reality. Last week, it was reported that Mr Ton-That, a college drop out and one-time model, used freely available academic papers and open sourced AI products to perfect a Facial Recognition algorithm. It was used in concert with billions of photos scraped from social networks to create a program that can be used to identify anyone from one single photo. His company, Clearview, has licensed his product out to Police departments across the United States.

Google and Microsoft both refrained from open sourcing their facial recognition algorithms for fear of this very thing happening. However, the overwhelming quantity of freely available products and expertise meant that their efforts were ultimately in vain. It’s unclear whether the open source movement will continue in the same vain but whether it does is unlikely to affect AI in the near future. Even products such as Google’s AI text-to-speech generator, which was debuted to much fanfare in 2018 at Google’s annual conference, has been recreated in open source by programmers even though the real thing remains propriety. What this means for the tech world is unclear, but its consequences could be dire. Hackers have already used AI to impersonate the CEO of a U.K.-based energy firm, running away with €220,000. When asked about whether large technology firms will be as committed to open source in AI as they are now, Mr Bengio replied: “I’m not really good at reading the future :-).”

]]> The Communication Workers Union (CWU), a postal union, have been accused of making a “politically calculated” plan to strike over the December election period, potentially jeopardising the counting of up to a fifth of votes over disputes regarding bonuses and pay.

The Union, who on Monday revealed its latest strategy was to refuse to deliver not only postal votes but also campaign material, are doing so in order to prompt a new agreement by Royal Mail on jobs and conditions.

The move has triggered many concerned conversations on the topic whether those not able to vote in their preferred constituency should cancel postal vote registrations and vote by proxy instead, fearing millions of votes going uncounted in arguably one of the most historic elections in recent history.

The potential strike could hinder Corbyn’s own chances at Number 10, potentially jeopardising the votes of thousands of students choosing to vote in home constituencies whilst still being at university, or vice versa.

However, due to the fact that almost half of Conservative voters are aged 65 or over, and the age group are amongst the most likely in society to request a postal vote, discussions have begun as to whether the move was politically timed to boost the Labour Leader’s chances at the ballot box.

This prompted Andrea Leadsom, the Business Minister, to suggest the CWU had indeed deliberately timed the potential strike.

“The timing of this threat is clearly politically calculated and would disrupt both the election and people’s Christmas deliveries,” she said.

She added the disruption would be “a taste of things to come” if Jeremy Corbyn is elected prime minister next month.

Each general election, Royal Mail postal workers are given compensation in the form of bonuses for the additional workload caused by postal votes and significant amounts of campaign material. During the 2017 election these bonuses were set at an average of £267 each, a figure the Communication Workers Union is demanding Royal Mail increases as part of wider industrial disputes.

The 140,000 members of the CWU voted overwhelmingly in favour of the strike, those in favour accounting for a staggering 97% of the vote. Their reasons for striking covered a range of topics outside of just bonuses, such as Royal Mail’s failure to uphold agreed deals on pensions and conditions drawn up last year.

In a statement issued by Royal Mail, they remained committed to ensuring the handling of postal election material “will be our number one priority.” Shane O’Riordain continued, “We will invest significant resource to seek to ensure a seamless process for the handling of postal election material. Royal Mail volunteers will also work on the processing and delivery of election mail.”

Regarding Royal Mail’s efforts to come to an agreement with the CWU, Mr O’Riordain detailed “Royal Mail is also meeting the CWU as a matter of urgency to discuss the union’s request for an increase in the dedicated payments postmen and women receive for delivering each poll card and individual item of candidate mail.” In addition, despite the growing frustration and anxiety surrounding the situation, no strike action has definitely been confirmed as of yet, “The recent ballot result for industrial action does not necessarily mean that industrial action will take place.”

However, concern is not wholly unwarranted, as in the 2017 General election over eight million votes were cast by post. Experts have warned that, should significant disruption occur, the final election result could be left open to legal action.

In addition to this, frustration over the supposed political interest of the CWU seems to have stemmed from the union’s General Secretary, Dave Ward’s close relationship to Jeremy Corbyn, claiming during the 2015 leadership campaign that Corbyn had to be elected to rid society of the “Blairite virus”.

Described as a lifelong socialist, Mr Ward has been an active union member since the 1970s, having joined Royal Mail at just 16 years old.

]]> 288 The Monocle takes a look at Student Lenders https://themonoclenow.com/politics/the-monocle-takes-a-look-at-student-lenders/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=the-monocle-takes-a-look-at-student-lenders Wed, 06 Nov 2019 16:41:22 +0000 https://themonoclenow.com/?p=292 Tom Saunders investigate Student Pay-Day Lenders and what they mean for Durham Students.

]]> Last month the Nation Student Money survey was released by Save the Student. Now in its sixth year it polled 3,385 students, making it the UK’s largest independent survey of its kind.

The report had some dire revelations, most noticeably that 79% of students polled worry about making ends meet – which is more students than ever before. Only 38% of students said that their maintenance loans stretched far enough.

Not all students are able to supplement their maintenance loan with money from their parents or an overdraft. Furthermore, contrary to what people outside university might think, many students simply don’t have time to take on a part-time job in addition to their studies.

This leaves students with few options in order to make ends meet. This is where commercial or payday loan companies come into play. The survey noted that the popularity of payday loans has increased from 2018 to 2019.

These payday loans are often exploitative, and many have begun to directly target cash-strapped students.

Many student unions in the UK have banned short-term lends from advertising on campus and have made active efforts to instead promote hardship funds instead.

When you type “Student Loan quick” into Google plenty of results come up. Many advertise loans from £100-£5,000 which can be agreed and paid in 15 minutes. One of the most prominent of these pay-day lenders is Smart Pig which describes itself as “the only short-term loan created just for students.”

Smart Pig is a perfect example of how an offshoot of the already morally questionable pay-day loan industry deliberately attempts to influence vulnerable students into loans which will often lead them into more debt.

On the page “Our Story”, Smart Pig mentions how the company is a student start up and that their founders “have a strong ethical focus …  and really want to improve practices and reduce prices in the short-term loan industry.” It is unclear exactly what Smart Pig means when it talks of its “strong ethical focus”.

The company has already been in trouble for advertising on beer mats, on the basis that it connected payday loans with drinking. It also was forced to scrap an ‘irresponsible’ poster offering a £3,500 prize because it encouraged students to take on debt. The poster told students that “Every customer is entered into our competition to win a term’s rent up to £3,500.”

Its founders, former Goldman Sachs trader and WBFF Amateur Fitness model Tom Parks and Shreiff Benazina, also own another short-term loan company called Buffa Loans. Buffa Loans features a picture of a mother and child prominently on its landing page and advertises loans with a representative APR of 1087%, which is more than double the 448% APR of Smart Pig. For comparison the APR on fixed rate credit cards is around 13% and for variable rate credit cards around 16%.

In a article in the Financial Times, Mr Benazina explained that “We don’t just want to do loans,” says Mr Benazina. “We want to help people find accommodation and practice for job interviews.” However, at the time of saying that Mr Benazina had been a Director of Buffa Loans, for over two years and seemed to be doing exactly that. The Smart-Pig About Page makes no mention anywhere of their other payday loan ventures and clearly attempts to appear as a solely student focused venture created by good natured students when in reality, they are quite far from that.

If you, or anyone you know, are facing financial difficulties or are unable to pay back a loan from a pay day lender you can contact your local Citizen’s Advice Bureau for help or take a look at DebtCamel which has advice about payday loans and how you may be able to apply for compensation. Durham University and some colleges also have financial support and hardship funds available to students, you can find more information here.
